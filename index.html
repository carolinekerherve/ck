Who I Am

I'm an International Board Certified Lactation Consultant, Certified Postpartum Doula and Clinical Psychologist.

I'm originally from France and arrived in San Francisco with my husband in 2006. I have two children (5 and 1 years old) and we currently live in Bernal Heights.

My job is my passion. Newborns are amazing and studies demonstrate time and again the abilities they have, especially with communication and reaction to their close environment: they are instantly active partners in the relationship. I am fascinated by early interactions between moms and babies. If there are some instinctive feelings and behaviors, moms and babies still have to adjust to them and learn to understand each other. They create their own language and mutually work on "attunement", or how parents react to their babies' moods and emotions. The simple way to hold or touch a baby makes the mom unique and has a lot of significance for her baby, who identifies her as knowledgeable, and above all caring and nurturing.

I have luckily found a way to use my previous skills in the field of psychology to explore such a fundamental area of human interaction. Because of my work as a psychologist, I know how to work confidentially, with respect for personal opinions, choices and histories. I work with partners and fathers as well. I am devoted to offering emotional support and practical information to new parents, and will do my best to adjust to each family's specific needs. I learn from every family with which I work.

F.A.Q.

A few things worth knowing

What are the benefits from having a postpartum doula?

  • continuity of care and attention after leaving the hospital
  • greater breastfeeding success
  • more confidence in parenting abilities
  • easier transition into parenthood
  • less postpartum depression and anxiety reactions
  • better communication
  • faster physical and emotional recovery

What does a postpartum doula do?

A postpartum doula works with each family individually to find out their particular needs. We offer many services to our families, but our main goals are to help “mother the mother”, and nurture the entire family as they transition into life with a newborn. This would include doing things to help mom and partner feel more confident in their roles, sharing education on family adjustment and tending to the unique needs of a new mother.

Services offered include: breastfeeding support, routine newborn care and instruction (including bathing, cord care, diapering, calming techniques, sleeping, newborn nutrition and development), moral support, household help (including laundry, light housekeeping, grocery shopping, and meal preparation), accompanying mother and baby to first outings or medical appointments.

Why would new parents choose to have a daytime doula over a night time doula?

Some parents can afford to have both. Some parents will prefer to stay close to their baby at night and the daytime doula will help them to nap during the day, so they stay well-rested. I tend to help families to find strategies to deal with common challenges, that won't imply to have someone coming for long hours and take over. I want to empower the parents I work with and allow them to go through those first weeks without feeling like they 'need' night help - if they don't have to or if they don't necessarily want to. I won't hesitate to suggest night support when there is a specific need though, due to familial circumstances.

When should I arrange for postpartum doula care?

It is best to arrange for postpartum doula care a few months before your estimated due date, so you can ensure the doula's availability and get to know each other beforehand, and be comfortable with this person in such an intimate and intense time. Doulas are also able to accommodate last-minute requests, depending on their schedule.

What is the difference between a Lactation Consultant, a Lactation Conselor, a Lactation Specialist, a Peer Counselor, an IBCLC? Is there even a difference?

There is a tremendous difference, mainly between IBCLCs and all others lactation professionals. IBCLCs (International Board Certified Lactation Consultants) are the premier providers or care for breastfeeding and human lactation. Rigorous defined eligibility requirements must be met in order to take the once-yearly certification exam conducted by the International Board of Lactation Consultant Examiners. Every IBCLC around the world must meet the same criteria to take the exam. Scores of hours of classroom-style instruction, and hundreds of thousands of hours of supervised clinical work solely in human lactation must be completed before one can take the exam. IBCLCs must recertify every 5 years and adhere to the IBCLE Code of Professional Conduct and Scope of Standards of Practice. (E. Brooks)
